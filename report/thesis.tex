\begin{abstract}
    \Glspl{vm} and containers are technologies that offer virtual computing environments, with their respective benefits of having strong isolation and being lightweight. For this reason, they are often used in similar, if not the same, contexts. However, since containers have only recently grown in popularity and \Glspl{vm} have been used since the early 1990s, they have traditionally been managed in separate management environments. This has led to a fragmented infrastructure for organizations that use both \Glspl{vm} and containers, but also to certain technologies and mechanisms having duplicate implementations, even though their implementations could have been shared. To remedy this issue, KubeVirt was created to unify the management of \Glspl{vm} and containers by integrating \Glspl{vm} into Kubernetes, a widely adopted platform originally designed for container orchestration. 
    
    This project aims to evaluate the applicability of managing \Glspl{vm} in Kubernetes, how it compares with traditional approaches and the implications of moving into a Kubernetes-based environment for \Gls{vm} management. A mixed-methods approach is used, including a literature study, a qualitative comparison in the form of a usability analysis and a quantitative comparison in the form of a performance analysis. 
    
    The results suggest that KubeVirt with Kubernetes offers a scalable alternative to traditional approaches, but that in certain use cases, KubeVirt might not be suitable. The results also show that the adoption and perceived usability of KubeVirt are tied to the experience of Kubernetes and its concepts.
\end{abstract}

\chapter{Introduction}
The earliest usage of virtualization goes back several decades, when larger computers were split into smaller units. This gave multiple users simultaneous access to a computer and allowed for more efficient use of hardware resources. However, it would not be until the late 1990s that virtualization would take off when commodity servers grew in popularity. This time, virtualization allowed companies to run a variety of applications on a wide range of \Glspl{os}\ \cite{virtualization-history}. This image of virtualization has led to its widespread adoption that is seen today, and nowadays virtualization forms the base for cloud computing, providing virtual environments that can be used for various workloads, including compute, network and storage\ \cite{virtualization-basics, virtualization-trends}.

The key enabler for virtualization is the hypervisor, which is a software layer that operates between the hardware and the \Gls{os} and is responsible for creating virtual computing environments called virtual machines (\Glspl{vm})\ \cite{virtualization-trends, virtualization-ibm, hypervisor-survey}. \Glspl{vm} are emulations of physical computers that provide a complete system with its own \Gls{os}. This means a \Gls{vm} is self-sufficient and can be isolated from its outside environment. This is particularly useful when running legacy applications and applications that require a high level of isolation. For these reasons, \Glspl{vm} have become the core part of IT infrastructure\ \cite{virtualization-history, virtualization-basics}. 

However, with the evolution of virtualization, an alternative to \Glspl{vm} was created called containers. By raising the virtualization level to reside above an \Gls{os}, containers become lightweight and portable\ \cite{container-vm-overview}. For this reason, containers have become a popular choice in scenarios where scalability is important, such as in a microservice architecture, which are core components in modern cloud-native applications\ \cite{cloud-native}. Figure\ \ref{fig:container-vm-architecture} visualizes the differences between how \Glspl{vm} and containers are virtualized, and how the virtualization layer is placed on a lower level for \Glspl{vm} than for containers. 

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[scale=0.96]{img/vm_container_virtualization.pdf}
    \caption{An overview of \Gls{vm} and container virtualization}
    \label{fig:container-vm-architecture}
\end{figure}

However, the benefits of a container do not come without a cost. Due to the fact containers share the underlying \Gls{os} kernel, it opens up a larger attack surface, which increases the risk of a security vulnerability. Some technologies have been created to mitigate this issue, such as Firecracker, Kata Containers and gVisor, but none of them has gained significant traction due to the trade-off they impose with additional overhead. This means the decision of using a \Gls{vm} or a container remains a use-case question\ \cite{container-runtimes-overview, container-as-vm-comparison}. 

Due to their different use cases, \Glspl{vm} and containers have traditionally been managed separately. There exists a wider range of management systems for \Glspl{vm} than for containers since they have existed for a longer time. \Glspl{vm} are often managed by a \Gls{vm} management system that provides tools to aid in life-cycle management. Many such systems are designed with a set of control nodes and worker nodes, where control nodes instruct the worker nodes with \Gls{vm}-related tasks, such as creating a \Gls{vm}. A \Gls{vm} management system can thus be seen as a hypervisor distributed across several nodes, which is illustrated in Figure\ \ref{fig:vm-management-system}. Examples of such systems include OpenStack\ \cite{openstack-website}, CloudStack\ \cite{cloudstack-website}, and OpenNebula\ \cite{opennebula-website}. Furthermore, a management system for a container is similar to that of a \Gls{vm}. It also provides a set of tools for life-cycle management and is commonly structured with control and worker nodes. However, a system that manages containers also includes certain operations that are not as applicable to \Glspl{vm}, such as auto-scaling. This has led to these systems being referred to as an orchestration system, rather than just management. Examples include Kubernetes\ \cite{k8s-website}, Nomad\ \cite{nomad-website} and Docker Swarm\ \cite{docker-swarm-website}, where the former is the most widely adopted in the industry \cite{k8s-it-administration}. As such, while VMs and containers have their respective use cases, they also share many aspects regarding how they provide virtual computing environments. For this reason, there has been an ongoing convergence in how \Glspl{vm} and containers are managed\ \cite{vm-cloud}. 

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[scale=1.1]{img/vm_management_system.pdf}
    \caption{An overview of a monolithic \Gls{vm} management system.} 
    \label{fig:vm-management-system}
\end{figure}

Recently, Kubernetes was extended to accommodate \Glspl{vm} through KubeVirt. It is a novel project that aims to fill a gap in how virtualized resources are managed by unifying the management of \Glspl{vm} and container into a single platform. Running both \Glspl{vm} and container in the same platform enables traffic to flow natively between the two resources, which is illustrated in Figure\ \ref{fig:container-vm-kubevirt-architecture}. How this is achieved on a technical level is discussed later in \ref{sec:kubevirt-technical-overview}. 

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[scale=1.05]{img/container_vm_with_kubevirt.pdf}
    \caption{An overview of \Glspl{vm} and containers managed by using KubeVirt}
    \label{fig:container-vm-kubevirt-architecture}
\end{figure}

The approach of managing \Glspl{vm} as a Kubernetes resource comes with many benefits. A \Gls{vm} can now be managed in a more cloud-native manner through Kubernetes workflows, but it can also integrate with tools that previously only supported containers\ \cite{cloud-native}. Additionally, managing both \Glspl{vm} and containers in the same platform reduces architectural complexity since only one platform is needed, as well as reducing the required skillset among the personnel\ \cite{kubevirt-cncf, kubevirt-storware}. 

The integration of \Glspl{vm} into Kubernetes using KubeVirt represents a significant milestone toward a more efficient and unified IT infrastructure\ \cite{kubevirt-storware}. However, KubeVirt's practicality in the real world is not yet fully explored. As such, this project aims to evaluate Kubernetes with KubeVirt as a \Gls{vm} management system and compare it with traditional approaches to understand its practicality and limitations.

\section{Problem}
\Glspl{vm} was the first technology that offered virtual computing environments that could be used to host various applications. This led to a widespread adoption of \Glspl{vm} and many management systems were since developed. With the evolution of \Glspl{vm}, containers were created as a lightweight alternative. However, this led to separate management systems being created for the two technologies, even though they are often used in a similar, if not the same, context. This means that duplicate implementations of mechanisms have been required, even though they could have been shared, but also that environments are more architecturally complex.

\section{Purpose}
The purpose of this project is to evaluate whether Kubernetes with KubeVirt is a viable option for \Gls{vm} management. This evaluation is important in the field of cloud computing since it can provide insight into how an existing system could benefit from integrating \Glspl{vm} management in Kubernetes, as well as by consolidating the management of \Glspl{vm} and containers to a single platform.

\section{Goals}
The goal of this project is to provide a transparent evaluation with quantifiable results regarding the applicability of managing \Glspl{vm} inside the Kubernetes ecosystem. The results could be used as a fundamental argument for or against Kubernetes as a management platform. Results that argue for the use of KubeVirt in terms of performance could be used as an argument for future research regarding sustainability.

\section{Research Questions}

    \\\\
    This could stem from using a single resource scheduler. Several metrics will be used to evaluate this, as described in the background and related work.

    \item What are the current challenges and limitations in managing \Glspl{vm} and containerized applications in separate environments?

\section{Research Method}
This project employs a mixed-methods approach that combines qualitative and quantitative methodologies. The approach provides a comprehensive and holistic approach to evaluating the applicability of managing \Glspl{vm} in Kubernetes.

\subsection{Literature Study}
The literature study explores the current knowledge related to \Glspl{vm} management, its history and how it is related to container management. It also explores Kubernetes concepts and how KubeVirt enables the running of \Glspl{vm} in an environment previously only used for managing containers. A mix of articles, technical reports and conference papers are used for the literature study. The selection of these is key to providing a foundational understanding that is necessary for the subsequent analyses. 

\subsection{Performance Analysis}
The quantitative aspects of the project stem from the performance analysis that evaluates KubeVirt with a traditional \Gls{vm} management system. The performance analysis measures the efficiency of each system in creating \Glspl{vm} and scaling the underlying infrastructure. It uses a programmatic approach that automates the running of tests and systematically collects metrics on both systems. The performance analysis involves the following steps:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Review existing literature that evaluates \Gls{vm} management system to identify benchmarking strategies.
    \item Design the testing strategy using existing research including metrics and payload size, and develop a testing tool.
    \item Perform the tests on KubeVirt and OpenNebula using the created tool.
    \item Gather and evaluate the results of the benchmarks to find possible differences between KubeVirt and OpenNebula in terms of performance and scalability.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Usability Analysis}
A qualitative evaluation of KubeVirt is conducted through a usability analysis. It involves having a group of system administrators perform a set of predefined \Gls{vm}-related tasks using KubeVirt, after which they are asked to provide feedback on the experience. The analysis involves the following steps:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Develop realistic scenarios a system administrator could face daily, and translate them into practical tasks.
    \item Create an environment that allows the participants to perform the tasks.
    \item Conduct the usability analysis with system administrators experienced both in managing a traditional \Gls{vm} management system and Kubernetes. 
    \item Gather and evaluate the feedback to identify possible advantages and challenges with using KubeVirt. 
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item This project only investigates what is available in KubeVirt and does not account for possible features that exist in a system that it is being compared to. This means that this project acknowledges that KubeVirt itself might not be a complete replacement for a traditional \Gls{vm} management system, but rather form a complete alternative in conjunction with Kubernetes tools and integrations.
    \item This project primarily focuses on the practical implications of moving into a KubeVirt-based system. However, it does not investigate or explore processes regarding the installation and configuration of a KubeVirt-based system.
\end{enumerate}

\chapter{Background}

This chapter provides all the fundamentals to understand the problem this project aims to solve. It includes fundamentals for what virtualization, hypervisors and containers are but also practical information regarding the currently used technologies. As such, this chapter provides the necessary information to understand why KubeVirt was created.

\section{Virtualization Fundamentals}
\label{virtualization}
Virtualization is the technology that enables hardware to be split or grouped into logical units. Each unit runs with its own allocated resources and is separated from other units running on the same host, which means virtualization can be applied to networks, storage and entire servers\ \cite{virtualization-basics}. The two former are simpler kinds of virtualization that involve creating an abstraction layer on top of existing physical resources to provide a logical interface defined in software. By defining storage and network in software, a more flexible environment is created, where an application interacts with interfaces that indirectly point to a group of hardware, instead of directly communicating with the hardware. Server virtualization, on the other hand, is the more common form of virtualization and is also known as hypervisor-based virtualization. It involves splitting a server into multiple virtual computing environments\ \cite{virtualization-history}.

A virtual computing environment, more commonly referred to as a \Gls{vm}, is a complete system that operates as an isolated environment. The environment is perceived as if it were an ordinary computer from the perspective of the application running inside it, even though the physical resources are shared with other \Glspl{vm} running on the same host. Being a complete system is particularly useful when running legacy applications that are dependent on a certain \Gls{os}, driver version or piece of hardware. Additionally, the isolated environments are suitable in scenarios with strict security requirements regarding the application running inside it\ \cite{virtualization-basics, virtualization-trends}.

The three major levels for virtualizations are hardware level, \Gls{os}-level and application level. Storage and network virtualization both fall under the hardware level category since they create an abstraction layer on top of the physical resources. However, virtualization is not required to be low-level. Application virtualization is concerned with encapsulating an application in a more lightweight environment, such that the application thinks it is executed on a specific platform. A common example is \gls{jvm} that enables programs written in the programming language Java to be run on multiple operating systems\ \cite{application-virtualization}. Figure \ref{fig:hardware-os-virtualization} illustrates this as an overview of the differences between the two more common layers of virtualization; hardware and \Gls{os} virtualization. It is important to note that the virtualization layers are not mutually exclusive and that using them in combination could lead to unique use cases. For instance, a guest process (\Gls{os} level) can run in a guest \Gls{os} (hardware level). This specific use case is referred to as nested virtualization\ \cite{virtualization-basics}.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[scale=1]{img/hardware_os_virtualization.pdf}
    \caption{An overview of hardware-level and \Gls{os}-level virtualization}
    \label{fig:hardware-os-virtualization}
\end{figure}

So far, virtualization has been described as being able to contain an entire environment virtually, but this is not always a feasible solution. While maintaining a low-level abstraction layer enables great flexibility, it could also lead to degrading performance\ \cite{virtualization-basics}. For this reason, virtualization was divided into two categories, full virtualization and paravirtualization. Full virtualization is what has been described so far and is the older and traditional approach where the entire underlying hardware is virtualized to its core. This allows a \Gls{vm} to portray any specifications to the application running inside it and the application has no way of knowing it is being run in a virtual environment\ \cite{virtualization-basics, xen-virtualization, hypervisor-xen}. Paravirtualization, however, reduces the number of resources that are virtualized by raising the virtualization layer slightly. With paravirtualization, a \Gls{vm} is presented with an image that is similar, but not identical, to the underlying hardware. A consequence of this is that the guest \Gls{os} is aware it is running inside a \Gls{vm}\ \cite{xen-virtualization, hypervisor-xen}.

\subsection*{Benefits of Virtualization}
Many of the benefits of virtualization stem from the fact that there exists an abstraction layer above the physical hardware. The abstraction layer decouples software from hardware, allowing for the use of vendors with different I/O in the same environment. This creates a more flexible and scalable computing environment as a whole where hardware is turned into a simple pool of computing resources\ \cite{virtualization-trends}. As such, there are numerous benefits of virtualization, a few of which are listed below.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Isolation}\\
    A \Gls{vm} provides isolation by being self-sufficient and containing its own \Gls{os}, including the filesystem and application stack. As previously described, being able to provide isolation is particularly useful in scenarios with high-security requirements\ \cite{vm-docker-reproducibility}.
    \item \textbf{Flexibility}\\
    Another consequence of being self-sufficient is portability, which leads to more flexible management of \Glspl{vm}. This is useful in many management-related tasks, such as live migration and load balancing, but also when performing maintenance on the physical hardware.
    \item \textbf{Resource utilization}\\
    A problem with larger servers is that hardware resources might not be fully utilized. \Glspl{vm} alleviates this issue by placing isolated workloads on the same physical hardware. If hardware resources are more utilized, it reduces the need to acquire additional hardware, which in turn could make server management simpler\ \cite{virtualization-basics}.
    \item \textbf{Management}\\
    A \Gls{vm} conceptually represents computing resources defined in software, which means they are by design manageable. This has led to \Gls{vm} management systems being created that allow remote control of \Glspl{vm}, such as creating and deleting them\ \cite{virtualization-basics}.
    \item \textbf{Virtual hardware}\\ 
    A \Gls{vm} is built upon virtualized resources, meaning resources that are not necessarily directly tied to a physical resource. For instance, a \Gls{vm} might be allocated 4 \Gls{vcpu} cores even though a server only had 2 physical CPU cores available\ \cite{virtualization-basics}. The benefits of this scenario specifically are described later in \ref{sec:overcommitment}.
\end{itemize}

\section{Understanding Hypervisors}
\label{hypervisors}
To allow a computer to be split into several virtual computing environments, a software layer is injected. This software layer is referred to as a hypervisor and is the key technology that enables the creation of \Glspl{vm}. Hypervisors are also referred to as \Gls{vmm}\ \cite{virtualization-history, hypervisor-survey}.

Hypervisors can be used in primarily two ways, either by having it manage its \Glspl{vm} using its own scheduler and memory management or by placing it as an abstraction layer on top of an underlying \Gls{os}. These are called \textit{type 1} and \textit{type 2} respectively. Type 1 hypervisors manage \Glspl{vm} by directly interacting with the underlying hardware. This means they are self-sufficient, but also that they are required to implement many features on their own. However, having no other middleware means type 1 hypervisor are performant and scalable. On the other hand, a type 2 hypervisor is situated above and \Gls{os} that enables its creation of \Glspl{vm}. This means a type 2 hypervisor delegates many mechanisms such as scheduling and resource management to an underlying \Gls{os}\ \cite{hypervisor-types-1-2}. While a type 2 hypervisor represents a simpler approach to creating \Glspl{vm}, it also inhibits a larger latency and overhead than type 1 hypervisors. For this reason, type 2 hypervisors have found their use cases in development and testing, rather than in production\ \cite{hypervisor-survey}. Figure\ \ref{fig:hypervisor-types} illustrates a high-level overview of the difference between a type 1 and type 2 hypervisor\ \cite{hypervisor-survey, hypervisor-types-1-2}.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[scale=1]{img/type1_type2_hypervisor.pdf}
    \caption{An overview of a type 1 and a type 2 hypervisor}
    \label{fig:hypervisor-types}
\end{figure}

Hypervisors can be further categorized into \textit{monolithic} and \textit{microkernelized} hypervisors\ \cite{hypervisor-comparion-2013}. Monolithic hypervisors are built as a single large software stack, managing all the \Glspl{vm} and their resources, as well as hosting drivers for all hardware access. Having all drivers reside within the hypervisor means that the hypervisor will only run on certain hardware. However, it also means no operating system is required at all, as the hypervisor acts as an operating system by supporting all the operations of its \Glspl{vm}. Microkernelized hypervisors, on the other hand, do not require device drivers to be present in the hypervisor. Instead, the drivers are installed in the form of a manager \Gls{vm}, often referred to as parent \Gls{vm}. Each \Gls{vm} created by such a hypervisor is thus referred to as a child \Gls{vm} since it uses the parent \Gls{vm}'s drivers to access the hardware. A microkernelized approach is more flexible than a monolithic, as it can allow the hypervisor to run on any hardware that supports the parent \Gls{vm}'s operating system. This distinction is similar to type 1 vs type 2 hypervisor, with one being more self-sufficient and the other more modular. However, it is important to note that monolithic and micro kernelized hypervisors are more concerned with the inner technical aspects of a hypervisor rather than the usability for the end-user.

The concept of \textit{security rings} is often used to describe the level of privilege different part of a hypervisor has. A security ring represents a hierarchical protection domain, where the innermost ring (ring 0), usually reserved for the \Gls{os} kernel, has the highest privilege, and the outermost ring (ring 3), usually reserved for user applications, has the lowest privilege. The hypervisor itself is often placed in ring 0, its \Gls{vm} kernel space in ring 1, and the \Gls{vm} user space in ring 3. Security rings are thus used to define execution level to provide a system to limit the execution capabilities of \Glspl{vm} to regulate access to shared memory. The abstraction leads to more transparency regarding the execution level of certain applications and is key for ensuring that a \Gls{vm} maintains its isolation\ \cite{hypervisor-comparion-2013, microkernel-monolithic}. Figure \ref{fig:hypervisor-security-rings} illustrates this concept as a layered group of rings.

\subsection*{Key Hypervisors}
\label{key-hypervisors}
There are several hypervisors available, each with its own set of features and capabilities, some of which are listed below.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{VMware ESXi}\\
    A type 1 monolithic hypervisor developed by VMware. It is often used in enterprise environments and is designed to use minimal system resources\ \cite{hypervisor-comparion-2013}. Being a type 1 hypervisor, it does not require an underlying \Gls{os} to run. 
    \item \textbf{Microsoft Hyper-V}\\
    A type 1 microkernelized hypervisor is used in the Window Servers suite. Its microkernelized properties stem from having a parent and child relationship between its components\ \cite{hypervisor-comparion-2013, hypervisor-hyper-v-esxi}. Being a type 1 hypervisor, it does not require an underlying \Gls{os} to run.  
    \item \textbf{Citrix Hypervisor}\\
    A type 1 microkernelized hypervisor that was previously known as XenServer. Its centralized parts consist of a modified Linux kernel called Dom0, which takes care of I/O operations and underlying hardware access, as well as creating other \Glspl{vms}, called DomU guests\ \cite{hypervisor-xen, hypervisor-comparion-2013}. Being a type 1 hypervisor, it does not require an underlying \Gls{os} to run.  
    \item \textbf{\Gls{kvm}}\\
    A type 1 hypervisor that is neither microkernelized nor monolithic. Instead, \Gls{kvm} is built into the Linux kernel and uses many of its functionalities such as the scheduler and memory management. \Gls{kvm} is also free and open-source, which has made it a popular alternative to proprietary hypervisors\ \cite{hypervisor-comparion-2013, hypervisor-kvm}. 
\end{itemize}

\section{VM Management Essentials}
To provide a control plane when managing \Glspl{vm}, many \Gls{vm} management systems were created. These systems comprise tools and mechanisms for managing the entire life-cycle of \Glspl{vm}, including creation, deletion and access. This makes a \Gls{vm} management system comparable to a hypervisor, in that they both manage a set of \Glspl{vm}. However, a \Gls{vm} management system operates both on a high level and on a larger scale by spanning over multiple nodes. As such, it often supports a wide variety of hypervisors among its nodes\ \cite{cloudframeworks-and-hypervisors}. However, certain hypervisor features, such as live migration, could have unique implementations for different hypervisors. This creates a possible trade-off for a \Gls{vm} management system where it can choose to implement fewer but homogeneous operations, or more tunable operations that implement hypervisor-specific features\ \cite{openstack-opennebula-comparison}.

\subsection*{Concepts and Features}
With a higher abstraction layer, a \Gls{vm} management system brings a set of concepts to simplify the management of \Glspl{vm}. Many such features and concepts exist and can be broadly grouped as clustering, availability and scalability\ \cite{cloudframeworks-and-hypervisors}. 

Clustering is a common mechanism found in \Gls{vm} management system that involves grouping a set of resources such that they are perceived as a bigger unit. This is useful in scenarios where a workload is too large for a unit. A \Gls{vm} management system can provide tools for grouping \Glspl{vm} in various forms. A common example of clustering computing resources is \Gls{hpc}, which is often used to process larger datasets by dividing larger computations into fewer but smaller units. However, clustering can also refer to placing a group of \Glspl{vm} behind a load balancer that distributes the load evenly\ \cite{google-hpc-overview,oracle-hpc-overview}. 

Availability is another concept tied to a system managing \Glspl{vm} and is concerned with how well a service can be kept running without disruption, even in the case of malfunctioning components. A common approach for ensuring availability is by providing \Glspl{az}, which is a type of design pattern where services are replicated in separate environments such that an environment can be kept running despite another one failing. For this reason, \Glspl{az} can span servers or even entire data centers\ \cite{cloudframeworks-and-hypervisors, azure-availability-zones}. Figure\ \ref{fig:availability-zones} illustrates a scenario in which \Glspl{az} provides redundant access to the same service. 

Scalability is a concept that is not directly tied to the \Glspl{vm} themselves, but rather to how well the system scales up or down. Fundamentally, a system can scale either by adding additional nodes or by increasing the resource capacity of the nodes, such as the number of CPU cores. These methods are called scaling out and scaling up respectively. Scaling out is commonly referred to as the more scalable of the two. This is because, in practice, there is a stricter upper limit on the performance that can be gained by adding hardware resources to a single node. A scale-out operation also causes less downtime since the responsibility is not as large per server. However, a possible argument against scaling out is that certain applications might not be designed to operate in several instances, such as a single-node database\ \cite{scale-up-out-case-study, scale-up-out-ibm}.

\subsection{Comparison of VM Management Systems}
This far, managing \Glspl{vm} has been described as being multifaceted, where a management system should provide availability and scalability. However, there are additional factors when deciding on which management system to use, such as its cost or required skillset to manage. 

In practice, there are mainly two types of systems offering \Gls{vm} management: public cloud and private cloud. A public cloud is a commercial platform entirely managed by a third-party company. These are characterized by being highly available and scalable, as well as having little to no up-front costs. A common denominator among public clouds is their pay-as-you-go model. A private cloud, on the other hand, refers to a piece of software that can be installed on a set of servers within an organization. This creates a clear trade-off where while an organization is responsible for managing servers and any data stored on them, it could lead to less expensive usage. Some of the most prevalent private cloud platforms are OpenStack\ \cite{openstack-overview}, CloudStack\ \cite{cs-overview} and OpenNebula\ \cite{openstack-opennebula-comparison}, and some of the most popular public cloud platforms are Amazon, Azure, and Google Cloud. A public cloud offers far more than just \Gls{vm} management. However, to keep the focus of the comparison, only the \Gls{vm} service of each public cloud will be brought up. These are Amazon EC2\ \cite{ec2-overview}, Azure Virtual Machines\ \cite{azure-overview} and Google Compute Engine\ \cite{gce-overview}.

\subsubsection*{OpenStack}
OpenStack is an open-source \Gls{iaas} platform that provides a large set of tools for managing virtualized resources. Its architecture is built to be modular using a component-based approach with each of the services it provides running as a separate component. The components communicate in a distributed manner using technologies such as message queues\ \cite{openstack-opennebula-comparison}\ \cite{openstack-overview}.

While OpenStack includes a large set of components, only the three major and most relevant to this project are brought up below.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Nova}\\ The \Gls{vm} service that provides a set of tools for managing the life-cycle of \Glspl{vm}, such as creating, deleting and migrating \Glspl{vm}. Nova is a relatively large component that is split into multiple parts each with a \textit{shared-nothing} and \textit{messaging-based} policy, meaning they are independent and communicate with other components using message queues. 
    \item \textbf{Swift}\\ The service that manages static data in a scalable and redundant manner. This is used by \textit{Nova} to store \Gls{OS} images and backups.
    \item \textbf{Glance}\\ The image service that provides a catalog of \Gls{vm} images that can be used when creating new \Glspl{vm}.  
\end{itemize}

In summary, OpenStack supports many features regarding \Gls{vm} management as well as a wide range of hypervisors. It is built to be efficient and scalable and has thus gained enormous traction in the industry\ \cite{openstack-opennebula-comparison}.

\subsubsection*{CloudStack}
CloudStack is an open-source \Gls{iaas} platform and cloud computing software that manages virtualized resources including \Glspl{vm}, virtual networks and storage resources. It is used primarily to build and manage private cloud environments\ \cite{cs-overview}. Architecture-wise, CloudStack is designed in a centralized fashion with a management server and agents. The management server can be viewed as a controller node and is responsible for sending out instructions regarding the life-cycle of \Glspl{vm}, such as creating, scaling and deleting them. The agent, on the other hand, is installed on each node designated to running \Glspl{vm} and takes care of low-level management tasks related to the \Glspl{vm}. The agent can thus be viewed as an intermediator between the hypervisor and the management server that enables CloudStack to be hypervisor agnostic. While CloudStack is not designed to be as modular as OpenStack, it is designed to be horizontally scalable and can be deployed in a distributed manner with multiple management servers\ \cite{cs-overview}\ \cite{cs-live-migration}.

CloudStack supports a large variety of hypervisors, such as VMware, \Gls{kvm}, and XenServer, as well as storage services\ \cite{cs-overview}. In summary, CloudStack provides an efficient public cloud environment similar to OpenStack. However, CloudStack does not scale to the same size as OpenStack due to its simple design\ \cite{openstack-opennebula-comparison}.

\subsubsection*{OpenNebula}
OpenNebula is an open-source \Gls{iaas} platform that is mainly used to build private clouds. Contrary to OpenStack, OpenNebula is built to be simpler with as little overhead as possible, which has made OpenNebula a common choice for larger-scale datacenters\ \cite{openstack-opennebula-comparison}. Its architecture is designed to be modular and is split into three parts as described below. \ \cite{openenbula-tele-teaching, iaas-comparisons}.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Tools layer}\\The high-level layer that provides a set of interfaces such as a \Gls{cli} and web-based user interface to interact with the system.
    \item \textbf{Core layer}\\Provides core functionalities to the system, such as host management, \Gls{vm} management and virtual network management.
    \item \textbf{Driver layer}\\The low-level layer that provides an abstraction on top of the underlying infrastructure, including networks, storage and hypervisors. Having this layer allows OpenNebula to support a wide range of providers, as well as adapt quickly to new technologies.
\end{itemize}

In summary, OpenNebula provides a simple yet efficient approach to \Gls{vm} management. Its simplicity and low overhead have made it a popular choice in the industry\ \cite{opennebula-website}.

\subsubsection*{Amazon EC2}
Amazon \Gls{ec2} is a service that provides \Glspl{vm} management in the public cloud platform \Gls{aws}, and it is used to host general-purpose applications\ \cite{ec2-overview, ec2-overview-web}. Since AWS is a public cloud platform, the technical specifications regarding hypervisor technology and such are not visible to the end user. \Gls{aws} provides a large set of templates that can be used when creating \Glspl{vm}, called  Amazon Machine Images (AMIs)\ \cite{ec2-overview}.

\subsubsection*{Azure Virtual Machines}
Azure Virtual Machines is a service provided by the public cloud platform Azure to manage \Glspl{vm}\ \cite{azure-overview, azure-cost-effective}. Similar to \Gls{aws}, Azure provides a set of preconfigured templates that can be used when creating \Glspl{vm} are chosen depending on the use case, such as general-purpose or \Gls{hpc} \cite{azure-overview-web}. Any technical specifications on the architecture of Azure Virtual Machines are not visible to the end user.

\subsubsection*{Google Compute Engine}
\Gls{gce} is a service provided by Google Cloud to manage \Glspl{vms} in the cloud\ \cite{gce-overview-web}. Similar to both \Gls{aws} and Azure, Google Cloud provides a set of templates when creating \Glspl{vm}\ \cite{gce-overview}. 

\subsubsection{Comparison}
\label{vm-management-comparison}
OpenStack, OpenNebula, and CloudStack are all examples of software that can be used to build both private and public cloud platforms. A private cloud is an on-premise cloud that is hosted within an organization and is, more often than not, dedicated to internal use. These systems are also often free and open-source, making them a go-to tool for many organization that seeks to host internal services. The systems are also highly configurable to work on most existing infrastructure. However, being highly configurable could be seen as a double-edged sword since it leaves much of the configuration of the system to the user, such as preconfigured \Gls{vm} templates.\ \cite{public-private-cloud, hybrid-cloud}. 

Amazon EC2, Azure Virtual Machines and Google Compute Engine are examples of \Gls{vm} management systems in public cloud platforms. They are designed with the end user in mind by providing a complete abstraction layer over the underlying hardware and hypervisors. This means that any configuration regarding the hypervisor and its settings is taken out of the equation. All three cloud platforms can be viewed as complete implementations of the previously mentioned private cloud platforms, including hardware. For this reason, the public cloud platform has seen enormous traction in the industry.
However, this also creates a trade-off where the user has less control and customization but instead gains a more user-friendly and complete system\ \cite{public-private-cloud}.

Private and public clouds both provide solutions for managing virtual resources and have their respective advantages. Public clouds scale better performance-wise since they make use of large data centers. However, it inevitably involves a third party which might not be suitable for certain use cases, such as for applications managing sensitive data. Private cloud, on the other hand, comes with a larger initial cost, both in terms of purchasing hardware but also to acquire the required skillset among the personnel managing the system. Which solution to choose depends on the specific needs of the organization, such as the level of control required, customization needs and security requirements\ \cite{hybrid-cloud}. 

\section{Containers and Kubernetes: A Paradigm Shift}
\label{containers-kubernetes}
To provide a lightweight alternative to \Glspl{vm} while still being able to create virtual computing environments, containers were invented. They provide environments with less overhead, which makes them excellent candidates for applications employing a microservice architecture. Microservices is a software design pattern that uses a set of small, independent and loosely coupled applications to provide a scalable service with minimal downtime\ \cite{microservices-overview}. Moreover, similar to \Glspl{vm}, containers are often managed using a management platform. Depending on the use case, many such platforms exist including Kubernetes\ \cite{k8s-website}, Nomad\ \cite{nomad-website} and Docker Swarm\ \cite{docker-swarm-website}, where Kubernetes is the most popular due its ability scale and integrate with other services. 

\subsection{Containers Explained}
\label{containers}
Containers are a form of virtualization that offers a lightweight alternative to \Glspl{vm}. Unlike \Glspl{vm}, containers do not require a hypervisor to run and instead run on top of an \Gls{os} and share the \Gls{os} kernel with other containers. By not requiring a hypervisor, containers can run with less overhead than \Glspl{vm}, making their life cycle relatively fast and efficient compared to \Glspl{vm}\ \cite{container-vm-overview, container-k8s-benchmark}. The differences between container and hypervisor virtualization are visualized in Figure \ref{fig:container-vm-architecture-2}.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[scale=0.96]{img/vm_container_virtualization.pdf}
    \caption{An overview of \Glspl{vm} and containers}
    \label{fig:container-vm-architecture-2}
\end{figure}

\label{dockerfile}
A container is created from a container image, which is a lightweight package that is used when configuring the virtual environment for the container. It encapsulates the application together with any dependencies such that it runs on any platform, which makes the container itself portable and consistent. The image itself is created using a configuration file called \textit{Dockerfile}\footnote{Recently, some movement has been made to change the name to \textit{Containerfile}, as this would describe the content better than the company that invented the technology. However, \textit{Dockerfile} is still the most widely used term.}, which describes a parent image, such as an \Gls{os} image, together with several steps required to produce the container image. Once the container image is created, it can be used as a parent image in any other configuration files, creating a chain of dependencies.

To enable caching, a container image can be divided into a series of layers. Each layer represents a change to the parent image, such as adding a file or installing a package, and thus acts as a checkpoint. This enables container images to be built incrementally, where layers are only rebuilt when a change is made\ \cite{container-docker-layers, container-dockerfile, container-docker-reproducibility}. Figure\ \ref{fig:container-image-layers} illustrates the concept of container image layers, where each operation represents a change to the parent image.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[scale=0.77]{img/container_image_layers.pdf}
    \caption{An overview of container image layers}
    \label{fig:container-image-layers}
\end{figure}

\label{container-registry}
Moreover, container images are often stored in container registries, which provide central storage and are used to distribute the images. A container registry often comes with tools to manage its images, such as tools for version control and scanning for malicious images. Another common event-based feature is webhook. It acts as a message broker for predefined events, such as notifying an external system that a new image was pushed. Together these features enable a container registry to integrate well in a cloud-native environment and development pipelines\ \cite{container-registries, container-registries-redhat}.

Originally, containers were designed to run on top of a technology developed by the company that invented containers called the \textit{Docker Engine}. Docker Engine is a container runtime that creates and manages containers similar to a hypervisor. However, to standardize an interface for container communication, the Open Container Initiative (OCI) was created, which defines a group of standards that aims to make containers portable across different platforms\ \cite{oci}. It is split into three standards called the runtime specification, image specification and distribution specification. From these specifications, the \textit{containerd} runtime was created, which aims to provide a more transparent and open-source alternative to the Docker Engine. Since it adheres to the standards, it allows for simpler integration with tools and services built on top of it. Today, many large systems use containerd under the hood, such as Kubernetes\ \cite{k8s-overview} 

\subsection{Orchestration Using Kubernetes}
\label{sec:kubernetes-orchestration}
Kubernetes is an open-source container orchestration platform that manages containers. It uses a set of abstraction layers to manage the life cycle of containers and all the communication between them. It provides a complete environment for the containers to run in, including scheduling, scaling and networking\ \cite{container-vm-overview}.

Kubernetes defines many abstractions to simplify container orchestration and communication, where the most basic ones are \textit{Pods} and \textit{Services}. A Pod is the smallest deployable unit in Kubernetes, which groups one or more containers into a single unit that can be run on nodes. This means that Kubernetes ensures all containers in a Pod run on the same host. The encapsulation allows coupled containers to reside on the same host and enables efficient communication between them. A common design pattern for containers in a Pod is the sidecar design pattern. It involves designating one or more containers to provide some additional functionality to a main container, such as logging or monitoring\ \cite{k8s-docs-pods, k8s-docs-sidecars}. A Service represents a communication entry point that is placed in front of one or more Pods. This means that a Service targetting a group of nodes acts as a load balancer, while a service that targets a single Pod is comparable to port-forwarding. Separating the communication into a separate layer is especially useful in scenarios where the Pods can serve client requests independently, and that the requesting client does not care which Pod it targets. Such Pods are referred to as \textit{fungible} Pods and are more common for cloud-native applications. Additionally, using Services in front of a group of Pods can lead to simpler deployment of updates, as the Pods can be updated one at a time using a rolling update strategy\ \cite{k8s-docs-services}. 

To enable a more consistent deployment of Pods, Kubernetes provides an abstraction on top of the Pods called \textit{Deployments}. A Deployment is given a desired state in which Pods should be and actively operates towards it. This means that if a Pod fails, the number of running Pods no longer matches the desired number of Pods, and the Deployment should create a new Pod. By following a desired state, Deployments are commonly used when the load of an application is irregular. For instance, a website might face heavier traffic during the day than during the night. In this scenario, the Deployment could be adjusted to scale down the number of Pods\ \cite{k8s-docs-deployments, k8s-overview}. 

So far, none of the scenarios have included a state that should be kept between or within Pods. Containers are inherently stateless by design, meaning that any data inside a container is lost upon deletion. Kubernetes primarily provides three abstractions to manage the state of an application: \textit{PersistentVolume}, \textit{PersistentVolumeClaim} and \textit{StatefulSet}. The former two are used in conjunction, where a PersistentVolume defines a storage target, such as the file path in a network storage server or on the local node, while a PersistentVolumeClaim acts as the intermediator between a PersistentVolume and a Pod. There is a one-to-one relationship between a PersistentVolume and PersistentVolumeClaim to ensure consistent access to the underlying storage, but also to provide a mechanism to notify Kubernetes that storage is no longer used\ \cite{k8s-docs-peristent-volumes}. A StatefulSet, on the other hand, is more comparable to a Deployment with a specific order for the Pods to start in. As previously described, a Deployment ensures that a desired number of Pods are running, but it does not guarantee the order in which they start or stop. A StatefulSet solves this by providing a more predictable strategy where each Pod has its own identity that is preserved between restarts. For this reason, a StatefulSet is often used for applications that define roles for a group of nodes, such as a database that must start a primary node before any secondary nodes\ \cite{k8s-docs-statefulsets, k8s-overview}.

Figure \ref{fig:k8s-overview} presents a brief overview of the Kubernetes abstractions and how they interact with one another. 

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[scale=0.83]{img/kubernetes_overview.pdf}
    \caption{Kubernetes core abstractions and how they communicate}
    \label{fig:k8s-overview}
\end{figure}
 
In some more sophisticated scenarios, these built-in abstractions might not suffice since they might not be able to model more complex types. For such scenarios, Kubernetes provides \Glspl{crd}, a form of extension, that allows user-defined types with logic and validation. For instance, a \Gls{crd} can define an abstraction similar to a Deployment, but it enforces certain sidecar containers to exist using custom validation rules. With \Glspl{crd}, the Kubernetes ecosystem can be tailored for specific use cases forming a more feature-rich environment\ \cite{k8s-crds, k8s-overview, k8s-crd-webiste}. Kubernetes includes many more abstractions, each with its use case. However, presenting an exhaustive list falls out of the scope of this project.

The design and workflow of Kubernetes resources are inherently declarative, meaning that instead of providing a set of instructions to move a resource into a specific state, a complete state is given in the form of configuration files called manifests. The manifest defines metadata, relationships and dependencies for a resource, and Kubernetes controllers are responsible for ensuring this state is applied and up to date\ \cite{k8s-docs-controllers}. Depending on the kind of abstraction, different logic is applied to ensure the resource specification matches the manifest. For instance, a Pod might be rescheduled to ensure it matches the manifest\ \cite{container-vm-overview, k8s-overview}. 

Architecture-wise, Kubernetes uses a client-server approach. The server, often referred to as the control plane, manages the state of the cluster, including scheduling and scaling, as well as networking. Since it consists of the API server, it can be viewed as the gateway to cluster, processing and acting upon the request of clients\ \cite{k8s-docs-components}. The client, often referred to as the worker node, receives Pod-related instructions from the control plane. Contrary to the control plane, a worker node consists of a container runtime, which allows it to run containers\ \cite{k8s-overview, k8s-docs-kubeproxy, k8s-docs-kubelet}. 

From an end user's perspective, Kubernetes resources are managed through the API server, which in turn notifies its workers. An end user can send requests using a \Gls{cli} tool such as \textit{kubectl} or using a programmatic interface through a REST \Gls{api}\ \cite{k8s-overview}. 

Through its abstractions, life cycle management and declarative client-server architecture, Kubernetes provides a complete and feature-rich platform for container orchestration. This has led to enormous traction in the industry and has Kubernetes a popular choice for container infrastructure\ \cite{k8s-it-administration}. 

\section{Containers vs. Virtual Machines}
Virtualization has brought many benefits within the realm of cloud computing by allowing hardware to be split or grouped into virtual hardware. It has been the key in enabling what is today referred to as cloud computing and cloud platforms. There are mainly two technologies that were made possible due to virtualization: \Gls{vm} and containers. \Glspl{vm} are complete and isolated systems with their own \Gls{os} and containers are a lightweight alternative to \Glspl{vm}. This creates a trade-off where depending on the use case, a \Gls{vm} might be more suitable or vice versa\ \cite{container-vm-overview, container-vm-comparison}. 

\subsection{Comparative Overview}
\label{container-vm-isolation}
The main difference between \Glspl{vm} and containers revolves around isolation and how independently they can run. As previously described \Glspl{vm} provides a complete system while containers share the \Gls{os} kernel with other containers. This can lead to more efficient resource sharing among the containers, but it also increases the attack surface of containers. A vulnerability found in the underlying \Gls{os} might compromise the containers running on the host. A \Gls{vm} reduces this risk by removing the \Gls{os} as a dependency\ \cite{container-kata-security}.

Isolation is also important when considering the scenario of co-location, meaning containers and \Glspl{vm} running on the same host. Since containers are allocated resources from the \Gls{os}, they are allocated as if they were ordinary applications. In many scenarios, this leads to efficient resource utilization, but sharing resources this way can be a double-edged sword. For instance, containers are more vulnerable than \Glspl{vm} to the noisy neighbor problem, since a co-located container could consume an unproportionate amount of the host's resources, which could lead to resource starvation among the other containers running on the host. Some solutions exist for this, such as the \Gls{os}-level feature \textit{cgroups}. A cgroup is a mechanism that limits, prioritizes and isolates resource usage for a process\ \cite{cgroups, vm-noisy-neighbor, container-vm-scale}. \Glspl{vm}, on the other hand, are not as susceptible to the noisy neighbor problem since they are allocated a static amount of resources from the host. However, it is important to note that a \Gls{vm} does not reduce the noisy neighbor problem for all resource types. For instance, unproportionate consumption of network traffic is still an issue for both \Glspl{vm} and containers\ \cite{container-vm-comparison}. 

\label{sec:overcommitment}
Overcommitment refers to a mechanism that allocates more virtual resources than what is physically available. This is a common practice when using virtualization, as hardware resources are often not fully utilized. Containers use mechanisms built into the \Gls{os}to achieve resource overcommitment, which involves multiplexing and virtual memory. However, resource overcommitment is more challenging for \Glspl{vm}. While CPU overcommitment is achieved using \Gls{vcpu}, memory overcommitment is solved by more sophisticated methods since a hypervisor allocates a static amount of memory to each \Gls{vm}. One such method is called memory ballooning and it involves sharing a subset of each \Gls{vm}'s memory with other \Glspl{vm}. It works by reclaiming memory that is not used inside a \Gls{vm} and allocating it to another \Gls{vm}. Then, when the first \Gls{vm} requires the now reclaimed memory, the hypervisor ensures the \Gls{vm} is either given back its memory or allocated a new chunk. As such, memory ballooning operates under the assumption that certain parts of the allocated memory are not used\ \cite{vm-ballooning}. However, it is important to note that a technique like memory ballooning does not directly solve memory overcommitment, but instead reduces the need for it\ \cite{container-vm-comparison, vm-overcommitment}. \label{container-vm-live-migration}
Live migration is a technique used to move a running resource to a new host without stopping and starting it. The technique is often used when balancing load over hosts or when performing maintenance. For \Glspl{vm}, live migration is a mature and widely used technique and works by copying memory pages from the source host to the destination host and then transferring the \Gls{vm} from the source hypervisor to the destination hypervisor. Since \Gls{vm} disks are often stored on shared storage, such as a \Gls{san}, the \Gls{vm} disk is not transferred but instead reattached after the live migration is finished. This usually requires some form of time-freezing to reduce the risk of data corruption. However, a live migration operation is usually fast enough to even notice\ \cite{vm-live-migration}. Live migration for containers, however, is not as mature as for \Glspl{vm}. A container requires \Gls{os}-level process migration, which is less reliable. However, several projects aim to provide live migration for containers, such as \Gls{crui}. \Gls{crui} works by checkpointing the state of a container, including its memory and file descriptors, and then restoring the state on another host. While this is considered as the defacto container live migration, it is more comparable to a snapshot operation than a live migration operation\ \cite{container-criu-overview, container-criu-linux, container-criu-web}. Another approach for live migrating containers involves terminating them on one host and restarting them on another. This strategy assumes the container is stateless or utilizes shared storage for its state, as is often used in Kubernetes since it does not provide container live migration. Instead, Kubernetes facilitates persistent storage through its persistent storage abstractions, such as PersistentVolumes and PersistentVolumeClaims. This means a user maps portions of the container's state to shared storage and assumes the rest can be discarded when a container is terminated\ \cite{k8s-overview}.

\subsection{Decision Factors}
The choice between containers and \Glspl{vm} depends on the specific use case, and there are several factors to consider when deciding which technology to use. Some of the core factors are security, scalability and legacy support\ \cite{container-vm-comparison}. 

As previously described, using a container implies a larger attack surface than for a \Gls{vm} due to sharing the \Gls{os} kernel. For this reason, \Gls{vm} have commonly been associated with stronger security since they are less prone to security vulnerabilities. This makes \Glspl{vm} an excellent candidate for application with stricter security requirements. Some examples include applications managing sensitive or confidential data\ \cite{container-vm-decision-redhat}. Another use case that is made possible due to a \Gls{vm}'s strong isolation is running unsafe or untrusted code. This use case is often referred to as sandbox and is useful when evaluating potentially malicious code that could otherwise risk harming the host system. Untrusted code could also involve user-submitted code, such as in GitHub pipelines that use \Glspl{vm} to execute actions based on some predefined event. This could be tests or some other development-related tasks created by a user\ \cite{gh-selfhost-runner-sec}. Using a \Gls{vm} in these scenarios ensures that the untrusted code does not affect the hosting system\ \cite{vm-decision-kong, sandboxing}

Configuration drift is another aspect to consider when deciding between using a \Gls{vm} or a container. It is a phenomenon that occurs when the configuration of a system changes over time, such that it could potentially lead to security risks. This is usually caused by continuous manual intervention in a system, where the summarized effects of all the changes made could be an unstable system\ \cite{configuration-drift-tripwire, configuration-drift-wiz}. This is more common for \Glspl{vm} than for containers, as a \Gls{vm}'s state is saved indefinitely. A container mitigates this problem by having its entire state defined in the Dockerfile (see\ \ref{dockerfile}), where its dependencies and configuration are more transparent to the person managing it. A container state is thus discarded once it is stopped, which allows containers to have a more consistent environment than \Glspl{vm}. This is further supported by the fact container registries (a central location for container images, see\ \ref{container-registry}) usually include image-scanning tools to find vulnerabilities in a container image itself\ \cite{container-vm-decision}. 

Scalability is a factor that is concerned with how well a system adapts to an increase or decrease in load. This can be measured in many ways where one being the overhead each additional computational unit has. Containers being lightweight and sharing hardware resources more efficiently implies they can scale up and down both quicker but also with a smaller resource footprint. However, as previously described, they are more susceptible to the noisy neighbor problem, where co-located containers could interfere\ \cite{container-vm-scale, container-vm-decision}. Moreover, scalability is also related to cloud-native applications, which are applications that are designed to run in the cloud\ \cite{cloud-native}. A cloud-native application is characterized by being horizontally scalable, stateless, and resilient, and is often built using microservices, see\ \ref{containers-kubernetes}. Cloud-native applications thus require small and lightweight environments making containers a natural fit.

Legacy support is another relevant factor to consider when comparing \Glspl{vm} and containers. It can be measured by how well a system can keep running old software or software depending on older hardware. In both cases, \Glspl{vm} is a good fit since they are a complete system. A \Gls{vm} can simply replicate the state of a legacy system, even including the hardware that was used. This allows an organization to acquire new infrastructure while still maintaining its legacy applications. Containers are also able to support legacy applications, but not to the same extent, as they are inevitably using a modern kernel\ \cite{container-vm-decision,vm-decision-microsoft}.

\subsection{Towards Stronger Container Isolation}
So far, containers have been described as having weaker isolation than \Glspl{vm} due to it sharing the underlying \Gls{os} kernel. However. several projects have been created to tackle this issue without completely resorting to a \Gls{vm}. These include Firecracker, Kata Containers and gVisor.

Firecracker is a \Gls{kvm}-based hypervisor that creates lightweight \Glspl{vm}, called microVMs. Compared to an ordinary \Gls{vm}, a microVM is much more lightweight and can start in a fraction of a second. Firecracker achieves such speeds through optimizations and a heavy stripped-down guest\ \cite{container-runtimes-overview, container-firecracker}.

Kata Container is another \Gls{vm} alternative that aims to provide a lightweight environment, while still having strong isolation properties. Unlike Firecracker, a Kata Container still uses a container, but wraps it using a lightweight version of a \Gls{vm}, which enables Kata Container to act as a runtime, such as containerd. This means Kata Container is simpler to implement than Firecracker since it can run ordinary containers\ \cite{container-runtimes-overview}. 

Another approach to strong container isolation is gVisor, an extension to a container that enforces stricter isolation by restricting system calls. This approach differs from both Firecracker and Kata Container in that it does not use a \Gls{vm} at all, and instead relies on intercepting system calls and file access requests\ \cite{container-gvisor-firecracker}.

In summary, these three technologies provide different approaches to the same problem. Firecracker is more \Gls{vm}-like, while gVisor is most similar to a container. However, due to the varying added overhead, they are yet to be widely adopted\ \cite{container-runtimes-overview}. \section{Extending Kubernetes with KubeVirt}
\label{sec:kubevirt}
Kubernetes was recently extended to accommodate \Glspl{vm} as a native resource through the KubeVirt project. KubeVirt is designed to integrate well with the Kubernetes ecosystem and provide a similar interface to \Glspl{vm} as any other Kubernetes resource. \Glspl{vm} created with KubeVirt are scheduled and communicate as ordinary Pods, allowing them to blend into the environment\ \cite{k8s-scheduler}. This means that a Kubernetes resource that integrates with a Pod can also integrate with a KubeVirt \Gls{vm}. For instance, Services can expose a \Gls{vm} externally and PersistentVolumeClaims can maintain its persistent storage. While KubeVirt uses Pods to wrap a \Gls{vm}, a \Gls{vm} abstraction is implemented as a \Gls{crd}. This allows KubeVirt to perform validation that is specific to a \Gls{vm}, such as referencing a non-existing disk\ \cite{kubevirt-architecture, kubevirt-validation}.

KubeVirt's design pattern follows Kubernetes conventions, using a declarative approach to managing the \Glspl{vm} and any other resources revolving around it, such as snapshots and live migration. A \Gls{vm} is split into a \textit{VirtualMachine} and \textit{VirtualMachineInstance}, where the VirtualMachine represents a desired state and the VirtualMachineInstance describes a running state. This relationship is similar to that of a StatefulSet and its Pods\ \cite{kubevirt-vmi}. Moreover, KubeVirt implements a \Gls{vm} controller to ensure the desired state is met, which is responsible for requesting an environment in which the \Gls{vm} can run, but also update the running \Gls{vm} when its desired configuration changes.\ \cite{k8s-docs-kubevirt, kubevirt-architecture}.

KubeVirt's declarative approach stretches to aspects that have been imperative operations in traditional VM management systems. The most prominent examples of this are live migrations and snapshots that are both requested using configuration files. As such, they are implemented as \Gls{crd} that triggers live migration or snapshot operations once created\ \cite{kubevirt-snapshot,kubevirt-live-migration}. This is a novel take on operations that traditionally have been imperative. However, the processes of triggering an operation upon resource creation have existed in Kubernetes for a long time. For instance, a storage device is connected to a Pod when a PersistentVolumeClaim is created\ \cite{k8s-persistent-volumes}.

A consequence of being built on top of Kubernetes is that KubeVirt-related resources can be managed using the same tools used for other resources, such as the \Gls{cli} tool kubectl. However, certain operations are still unique to \Glspl{vm} and are not yet supported by kubectl. This includes accessing a \Gls{vm} over \Gls{ssh} or \Gls{vnc}, which are inherently imperative operations. For this reason, KubeVirt provides a complementary \Gls{cli} tool that implements these missing features\ \cite{kubevirt-virtctl}.

\subsection{KubeVirt Technical Overview}
\label{sec:kubevirt-technical-overview}
On a technical level, KubeVirt does not implement a new deployable unit for their \Glspl{vm} and was instead designed to encapsulate them using Pods (see \ref{sec:kubernetes-orchestration}). This design has allowed KubeVirt \Glspl{vm} to integrate well with other Kubernetes resources such as Sevices and PersistentVolumeClaims. 

KubeVirt can be split into four core components: \textit{virt-controller}, \textit{virt-handler}, \textit{virt-launcher} and the \textit{libvirt daemon}. The virt-controller and virt-handler are both concerned with managing the life cycle of the \Glspl{vm}, where the virt-controller is responsible for creating the Pod for the \Gls{vm} and the virt-handler is responsible for creating the \Gls{vm} itself. The virt-launcher manages the runtime environment for a \Gls{vm} making it responsible for requesting start and stop operations, as well as monitoring. Finally, the libvirt daemon (libvirtd) provides the interface to manage the \Glspl{vm} hosted by the underlying \Gls{kvm} hypervisor. The relationship between the three components is illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:kubevirt-components}, where the virt-controller operates closest to Kubernetes \Gls{api}-server, virt-handler operates on every node to communicate with the underlying hypervisor, and virt-launcher operates as a sidecar container in every Pod to manage the runtime environment of the \Gls{vm}\ \cite{kubevirt-architecture, k8s-docs-kubevirt}.

\subsection{KubeVirt and GitOps}
GitOps is a set of practices that enables using Git as a storage for the desired state of a system. Using a storage managed by Git allows resources to be version-controlled and audited. This approach keeps developers and maintainers in focus since it allows their resources and codebase to exist in the same system. For some scenarios, the resources managed in Git might even be the same resources that host their application\ \cite{gitops-evolution}.
For this reason, GitOps becomes a natural fit for KubeVirt since \Glspl{vm} are managed in files\ \cite{gitops-kubevirt}.

\subsection{Comparison with Traditional Approaches}
So far, KubeVirt has been presented as a silver bullet for \Gls{vm} management. However, it is important to note that KubeVirt does not represent a new virtualization technology, but rather a unifying technology. Behind the hood, KubeVirt uses \Gls{qemu} and \Gls{kvm} to create the underlying \Gls{vm}, which is similar to any other traditional \Gls{vm} management system. This means that KubeVirt should in theory provide the same level of isolation and performance as any other management system. However, KubeVirt does fall short in that the \Gls{vm} is encapsulated inside a Pod, as this could lead to a performance overhead. Another aspect to highlight is that KubeVirt itself is not directly comparable to existing \Gls{vm} management systems, but rather forms a fully-fledged system when evaluated together with Kubernetes. For example, KubeVirt's implementation does not implement its scheduler or storage solution and instead relies on Kubernetes implementation and integrations, while OpenStack has its own scheduler as well wide support for different storage solutions including data warehousing\ \cite{kubevirt-tech-comparison, kubevirt-architecture, kubevirt-network-plugin, kubevirt-disks}.

\section{Related Work}
\label{literature-study}
\subsection{Current challenges}
The \Gls{cncf} presents in a report some of the current challenges of managing \Gls{vm} using traditional approaches, such as an \Gls{iaas} platform\ \cite{kubevirt-cncf}. They argue that a platform such as OpenStack is overly complex and difficult to manage for an organization that only wants to manage \Glspl{vm}. They conclude by suggesting KubeVirt with Kubernetes as a simpler alternative to \Gls{vm} management.

Moreover, the \Gls{cncf} report also mentions the potential benefits of a unified platform for both \Glspl{vm} and containers. They argue that a unified platform reduces the required skillset and management burden for personnel, as well as reducing the architectural complexity of the system. These benefits are supported in a report by Kubacki\ \cite{kubevirt-storware}. He argues that managing both a traditional \Gls{vm} management system and a Kubernetes could lead to unnecessary costs for an organization. Additionally, he argues that by allowing \Glspl{vm} and containers to exist in a single platform \Gls{vm}-only organizations could potentially be driven toward adopting containers for suitable workloads. 

\subsection{KubeVirt challenges}
While KubeVirt is presented as a solution to many challenges related to managing \Glspl{vm}, it is important to consider the challenges that come with KubeVirt. A study by Trakulmaiphol and Piromsopa presents some shortcomings when moving into a KubeVirt-based system and found some issues related to how networking\ \cite{kubevirt-migrate-issues}. One of the issues was related to how IP address management differs in Kubernetes compared to traditional \Gls{vm} management systems. For instance, static IP addresses were difficult to assign and manage. In the study, they developed a framework to solve some of the issues on the application level, such as DNS queries, and provided a set of guidelines related to KubeVirt's unique features including the Multus Container Network Interface. 

A common practice when managing Kubernetes clusters is to deploy Kubernetes nodes on \Glspl{vm} created by a \Gls{vm} management system. This means that when using KubeVirt, \Glspl{vm} are created inside other \Glspl{vm} leading to nested virtualization. Ye et al.\ argue in a study that nested virtualization raises security concerns since it increases the size of the \Gls{tcb}\ \cite{free-turtles}. A larger \Gls{tcb} means that the size of the system's attack vector increases, which in turn leads to an increased risk for vulnerabilities. This study is particularly relevant as it highlights a potential limitation if KubeVirt is simply seen as another Kubernetes resource and not as a \Gls{vm} management system. 

\subsection{Usability of Kubernetes and KubeVirt}
Using Kubernetes as a \Gls{vm} management system is a relatively new concept and there is limited research on the usability of KubeVirt. However, several studies have evaluated the usability of Kubernetes, which is equally relevant since KubeVirt is built on top of Kubernetes.

Kubernetes \Gls{api} is inherently declarative, meaning it uses a configuration to describe a desired state it should be in. A study by Sonninen investigates many of the possible benefits of such an approach\ \cite{declarative-benefits}. The study found that a declarative approach enables a system to use GitOps, which is a way to manage infrastructure using files in storage backed by Git. This simplifies resource management through its built-in version control and audit, as well as rollbacks (given that it is supported by the resource). Two studies exemplify these benefits when presenting their case study on when CERN migrated an internal project from a cluster of \Gls{vm} to Kubernetes. The first study conducted by Imran et al.\ describes how migrating to Kubernetes improved the overall system management compared to using a \Gls{vm} cluster\ \cite{k8s-cern}. While many of their experienced benefits came from adopting containers instead of \Glspl{vm}, the study still suggests Kubernetes itself provided many benefits, such as reduced operational costs. The other study conducted by Badran presents the practical benefits they experienced by using Kubernetes and is more focused on the adoption of a declarative approach for system management\ \cite{k8s-cern-techreport}. It describes how using Kubernetes instead of a \Gls{vm} cluster allowed them to create reusable configurations that enabled their entire infrastructure to be managed as code. 

The benefits of moving into Kubernetes are further exemplified in a study by Gelley\ \cite{k8s-cloud-foundry}. It presents a case study where an organization moved its application from a \Gls{paas} provided by Cloud Foundry into Kubernetes. The study not only describes how Kubernetes provides more flexible configuration and deployment compared to the previous \Gls{paas} but also how the organization was able to make use of the tools Kubernetes provides to enable auto-scaling and monitoring. Moreover, the study concludes that by moving into Kubernetes, possible future migration to other platforms was made simpler due to how widely adopted Kubernetes is. 

User experience in Kubernetes was further investigated in a study by Packard et al.\ that collected several projects that moved into Kubernetes\ \cite{k8s-user-experience}. The study covered many aspects, including administration such as managing persistent storage and quotas, but also developer experience such as deploying applications. The study found that system administrators in general face a relatively steep learning curve when using Kubernetes since they need to learn many of the concepts that make up an application. Interestingly, it also found that many users without prior knowledge of Kubernetes could perform basic tasks without any major issues, such as deploying an application. This finding is especially useful when evaluating KubeVirt since the ability to deploy a built-in resource in Kubernetes is similar, if not identical, to deploying a KubeVirt-based resource. Another study that investigated the user experience of Kubernetes was conducted by Boronin\ \cite{k8s-migration-king}. The study presents a case study where an organization migrated from a monolithic system design to a more cloud-native design. The study is relevant as it is not only concerned with the technical aspects of Kubernetes but also considers the organizational impact of migrating to Kubernetes. For instance, transferring skill sets among personnel and the impact on the organization's culture.

\subsection{Performance Evaluations}
Regarding performance, several studies have evaluated popular systems that can manage \Glspl{vm}, including open-source alternatives such as OpenStack and CloudStack, or proprietary systems such as Amazon EC2. 

A study by Kang et al.\ compared the performance of OpenStack and Amazon EC2 using a wide range of configurations and analyzed diverse aspects based on different hypervisor, storage and networking configurations\ \cite{openstack-ec2-comparison}. Apart from the performance comparisons, the study also investigated performance impact when using more advanced features, such as Infiniband networking instead of Ethernet. The study showed mixed results, where OpenStack outperforms EC2 in some scenarios such as when using Infiniband networking, but EC2 tends to outperform OpenStack in parallel processing. As such, the study concluded that component-wise, each system has strengths. This study brings with it relevant perspectives as it highlights many of the aspects that are important to consider when evaluating the performance of \Gls{vm} management systems.

Another study by Mullerikkal and Sastri compared OpenStack and CloudStack regarding implementation complexity, stability and performance\ \cite{cs-os-comparison-2009}. Compared to the previous study, these performance tests were designed to measure disk and network I/O, and not CPU or memory usage. The study found that OpenStack was significantly more complex than CloudStack, but that it was overall more stable. The study also found that OpenStack had a slightly but overall better performance than CloudStack. These claims are further supported by another study conducted by Paradowski et al.\ \cite{cs-os-comparison-2013} a few years later that also compared CloudStack and OpenStack. The study benchmarked the two systems in terms of practical tasks such as time to deploy a \Gls{vm} and resource utilization during the deployment. The study found that OpenStack was slightly faster in most operations and had a lower resource utilization. While the tests were designed to represent user interactions, their scale was relatively small and did not explore any limitations of either system. 

In contrast, a later study by Amaral benchmarked KubeVirt uses a setup with a large number of \Glspl{vm} relative to the size of the nodes, aiming to explore the limits of KubeVirt's control plane (scheduler etc.)\ \cite{ibm-kubevirt-scale}. The study found that, while KubeVirt was able to create all \Glspl{vm}, the control plane suffered a heavy load, indicating a possible large overhead when KubeVirt provisions a large number of \Glspl{vm}. This finding is important to consider when evaluating KubeVirt, as it indicates that KubeVirt may not yet be suitable for managing a large number of \Glspl{vm}.

In terms of network performance, a study conducted by Kolahdouzan investigated the networking traffic pattern in KubeVirt and OpenStack by performing networking performance tests to evaluate potential \Gls{vm} overhead\ \cite{kubevirt-openstack-network}. The study found that the network overhead was significantly larger for scenarios in which \Glspl{vm} reside on separate nodes. However, while the study found overhead for both KubeVirt and OpenStack, it found that OpenStack had a considerably larger overhead. While this project will not focus on networking performance, it is important to consider the findings of this study when evaluating KubeVirt.

So far, only related work that evaluated higher-level systems has been presented. However, several studies have evaluated the performance of hypervisors. A study by Hwang et al.\ evaluated the performance of a range of hypervisors including \Gls{kvm}, Hyper-V and Xen\ \cite{hypervisor-comparion-2010}. The study did not manage to draw any conclusions on which hypervisor was most performant and instead concluded that it depended on the specific use case. This conclusion is interesting as it suggests that the performance of a hypervisor could be an obsolete factor when deciding which one to use. Either way, reviewing hypervisors is important when evaluating KubeVirt, as KubeVirt uses \Gls{kvm} as its hypervisor.

\section{Building a Framework}
A \Gls{vm} management system is often comprised of multiple services often running on separate nodes, and this project is no different since it uses a multi-node setup for the tests. This means that to accurately evaluate the performance of a \Gls{vm} management system, all nodes should be considered. This is a tedious task if done manually, and thus a framework was created that automates the entire process of installing a management system. This includes installing and configuring the management system, running the tests and cleaning up. The framework was designed to be \Gls{vm} management system agnostic, meaning any management system that supports the functionality defined in an interface can be evaluated using the framework. Consequently, this means the framework in theory can evaluate any locally installed \Gls{vm} management systems, such as CloudStack and OpenStack.

\subsection{Design}
\label{sec:framework-design}
Modularity and loose coupling are core aspects of the framework's design and make the framework self-sufficient. By not being dependent on external libraries and tools, the framework is easily installable and tests can be run with little to no effort. However, it is important to note that since the framework relies on computing resources hosted in Azure, running any number of tests can incur a cost. One of the components that enables loose coupling is an interface that defines a set of operations that \Gls{vm} management systems implement and that tests can use. By restricting the operations to those defined in the interface, the tests become more consistent and fair. 

Each implementation is given a state consisting of metadata for hosting nodes, such as name and IP address, to execute the operation. This means each implementation can communicate with the hosting nodes however desired. The implementations for KubeVirt and OpenNebula both use \Gls{ssh} as a base and send commands to the control node over a \Gls{ssh} tunnel. The full definition of the interface is found in the Appendix\ \ref{appendix:vmm-interface}

As previously described, metrics are collected before, during and after the test is completed. This poses a challenge where a bad implementation of a metrics collector can introduce noise in the measurements. To minimize the effect of the metrics collector itself, the framework relies on a daemon running locally on each hosting node. A daemon is a service that acts like a detached process, running as long as its host does. However, to reduce unnecessary burden on the hosting nodes, metrics are only collected when a test is active. This is controlled by placing an empty file called \textit{run} in a specific directory. The daemon simply checks for the existence of the file and starts collecting metrics accordingly. The metrics are collected on each hosting node by the daemon that stores a \Gls{json} file locally with all its collected data in a predefined format. After each test is finished, the framework stops the daemon from collecting metrics and then downloads its output file. This approach reduces the number of required interactions with each hosting node down to zero since the metrics are collected after the test is finished. 

On a technical level, the metrics are collected using a set of tools installed on each hosting node, which are installed before any test is run. The choice of tools was based on how standardized they are, as well as general availability to ensure future attempts to reproduce the results are possible. Table\ \ref{table:metric-tools} shows an overview of tools the daemon uses. Since the cumulative delay among the tools was substantial, the daemon fetches metrics from each tool asynchronously. Additionally, since each tool produces an output with its unique format, some additional tools are used to parse the outputs, such as \textit{awk} and \textit{grep}. The daemon is written in Bash to ensure that it can run on any Linux distribution and does not require any external dependencies. The code for the daemon can be found in Appendix\ \ref{appendix:metrics-collector} and the framework design is illustrated in Figure\ \ref{fig:framework-design}.

The framework's flow is split into four phases to allow for both caching and modular control. This is useful when developing the framework, but also when running the tests, as the environments do not need to be deleted between each iteration. Skipping a phase is controlled by editing a configuration file. However, it is important to note that certain phases cannot always be skipped. For instance, if an environment has been torn down, the next iteration must both recreate and reinstall each hosting node. While skipping a clean-up phase can introduce noise in the results due to potential caching functionalities, it also reduces the cost of the hosting nodes in Azure. The entire workflow is illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:framework-workflow} and is described below.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Setup}: The setup phase is responsible for creating the nodes and networking in Azure. It is also responsible for installing base packages that every node requires, such as \textit{sysstat} to collect utilization metrics and \textit{jq} to parse JSON output.
    \item \textbf{Installation}: The installation phase is responsible for installing and configuring the \Gls{vm} management system on the Azure nodes. Each \Gls{vm} management system implements an installation method that is responsible for installing and configuring the system with one control node and two worker nodes such that it can run a \Gls{vm}. The installation method also installs several standby workers that will be used when scaling up and down the number of worker nodes. By having standby workers, the framework can independently measure the cost of scaling up and down without the cost of downloading dependencies. While larger dependencies can be seen as a metric on their own, it is important to note that production environments usually generate \Gls{os} images with prepared dependencies, rendering the cost of downloading dependencies irrelevant.
    \item \textbf{Benchmark}: The benchmark phase is responsible for running the performance tests. The tests are described in\ \ref{table:performance-tests} and are designed to evaluate the general performance of the \Gls{vm} management system. Each test is a combination of the methods the framework interface offers, such as creating \Glspl{vm} in different sizes, migrating \Glspl{vm} between nodes and scaling up and down the number of worker nodes. This phase generates and saves the results of the tests in a JSON file.
    \item \textbf{Teardown}: The teardown phase is responsible for deleting the Azure nodes and ensuring that no resources are left behind. Having this as a separate phase allows for tests to run sequentially without the time cost of creating and deleting nodes for each test. 
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Implementation}
On a technical level, the framework is implemented using the programming language Go. This choice was made due to its performance but also its concurrency features since many tests involve asynchronously making requests to a \Gls{vm} management system. 

Furthermore, the most important part of the framework is arguably to provide fair and reproducible comparisons. As described previously, each \Gls{vm} management system implements an interface that defines methods for creating and deleting \Glspl{vm}. While this provides uniform operations from the framework's point of view, it also makes the method's implementation crucial: KubeVirt's implementation of creating a \Gls{vm} should be as close to OpenNebula's implementation as possible to ensure that the comparisons are fair and that no unnecessary over head is introduced. One example of this is that any network-related configuration is omitted since network-related metrics are not considered in the project. However, all features that a \Gls{vm} management system offers and is beneficial for the specific operation are considered. For instance, OpenNebula does not allow a live migration action to be performed on a \Gls{vm} that is not running, while KubeVirt allows the operation to be requested despite the state of a \Gls{vm}.\ This allows KubeVirt to omit the waiting time for a \Gls{vm} to be in the correct state before requesting a migration action.

\chapter{Usability Analysis}
The qualitative aspects of this project are derived from a usability analysis, which is comprised of a set of tasks designed in cooperation with a system administrator at Net Insight. The goal of the usability analysis is to mimic tasks that a system administrator can face on a daily basis. The tasks are designed to be done using the Kubernetes \Gls{cli} tool kubectl since KubeVirt does not come with a \Gls{gui} like a traditional \Gls{vm} management system, and using a third-party alternative could risk introducing bias that is not directly tied to KubeVirt. 

\section{Setup}
The usability analysis was hosted on a managed Kubernetes cluster in Azure (AKS). Similar to the performance analysis, this approach was chosen due to its availability. This means that the participants could perform the tasks anywhere at any time. Additionally, by not being required to install a local Kubernetes environment, the participants could focus on the tasks rather than the infrastructure.  Moreover, AKS provided predefined storage options that allowed for simple management of persistent disks and snapshots, which was useful to keep the focus on a specific operation, rather than the required configuration and infrastructure. Similar to the performance analysis, the node setup was crucial to enable certain operations such as live migration that involves moving a \Gls{vm} between two worker nodes. For this reason, a setup consisting of one control node and two worker nodes was used. Feedback from the participants was collected from the participants either by answering a set of open-ended questions or as a summary of a group of tasks. 

\section{Task Design}
To ensure the focus is kept on the usability of KubeVirt, the analysis is structured as a step-by-step guide split into three tasks. This allows the participants to familiarize themselves with KubeVirt before moving on to more complex tasks. The tasks are described below. 

\begin{itemize}
    \item\textbf{Basic usage}\\
    The first task is designed to act as a starting point for the usability analysis. The participants are asked to create a single \Gls{vm} configured with a \textit{containerDisk}, connect to it using SSH and \Gls{vnc} and then delete it. The task acts as a starting point for the participants and its purpose is to evaluate how well KubeVirt supports the most basic operations. 

    \item\textbf{Maintenance}\\
    The second task dives into maintenance-related tasks, which are recurring tasks a system administrator might face when maintaining a set of \Glspl{vm}. The task can be divided into two parts: live migration and snapshots. The first part involves creating a \Gls{vm} on the first worker node, then using KubeVirt's mechanisms for live migration, moving it to the second worker node without any downtime. The second part of this task is concerned with creating and restoring a snapshot of a \Gls{vm}. A snapshot of a \Gls{vm} represents the state of a \Gls{vm} at a given time point. Both live migration and snapshotting are included in the usability analysis since they are applied differently in KubeVirt compared to traditional \Gls{vm} management systems due to KubeVirt's declarative approach, see\ \ref{sec:kubevirt}.

    \item\textbf{Debugging}\\
    The third task is concerned with debugging and how well KubeVirt allows a user to resolve potential configuration errors. As described in\ \ref{sec:kubevirt}, KubeVirt is implemented using \Glspl{crd}, which has built-in field validation to prevent misconfiguration. However, the validation does not stretch to include runtime issues, such as cloud-init configuration mismatches or certain typos. This task is thus designed to present the user with a \Gls{vm} that has a cloud-init configuration error causing it to not be accessible over \Gls{ssh}. In this scenario, the participants have to find an alternative entry point to the \Gls{vm} to debug the issue or resolve the issue immediately by inspecting the configuration file.
\end{itemize}


\section{Conducting the Usability Analysis}
A group of system administrators at Net Insight were asked to perform the tasks presented above. They were chosen primarily by their role and experience with OpenNebula as well as Kubernetes. This provided a mix of knowledge among the participants, which reduced the risk of biases. The participants were given separate namespaces, a kubeconfig file to access the Kubernetes cluster and then presented with the tasks in the form of a PDF document. By giving each participant its namespace, it reduced the risk of interference between them. Moreover, the participants were given two weeks to complete the tasks, which allowed plenty of time to familiarize themselves with KubeVirt and potentially explore areas or features around it, including its documentation. The entire usability analysis can be found in the Appendix\ \ref{appendix:usability-tasks}.

\chapter{Discussion}
The goal of this project was to evaluate the applicability of managing \Glsl{vm} in Kubernetes using KubeVirt, and by using a mixed-methods approach, this project has produced quantitative and qualitative results. This chapter will provide additional insight by interpreting the results and concluding whether the research questions can be answered. 

\section{Limited Features and Result Intepretation}
\label{result-intepretation}
The performance analysis was created to explore any quantifiable difference between managing \Glspl{vm} using KubeVirt and using a traditional approach. Since \Gls{vm} management systems are complex and span across multiple nodes, a framework was developed that included a common interface that a management system could implement to be benchmarked. This approach is modular and enables other \Gls{vm} management systems to be benchmarked by adding an implementation for it. 

However, this creates a flaw where, depending on the implementation, a \Gls{vm} management system can appear to be more or less efficient than it is. This means that, by definition, such a framework is only as good as the implementations are, which is why the implementations for KubeVirt and OpenNebula were designed as close to each other as possible. However, certain features and settings were not able to be tuned in the same way since they did not exist at all in both systems, such as the scheduling configuration where OpenNebula provides only a single option for scheduling: the number of \Glspl{vm} that can be dispatched concurrently. If both systems are not able to be tuned in the same way, it leaves the results for certain tests open for interpretation.

\newpage
\noindent To favor a system that is more tunable and configurable, this project has opted for an approach where if a system does not provide a specific setting, that setting is assumed to be fully optimized. The primary example of this can be found in the \textit{Create Many Type-1 \Glspl{vm}} tests, see Figures\ \ref{fig:create-many-cpu} and\ \ref{fig:create-many-duration}. KubeVirt, using the Kubernetes scheduler, was able to create 20 type-1 \Glspl{vm} in 63 seconds, while it took OpenNebula 157 seconds on average. However, the results also suggest that KubeVirt uses more resources than OpenNebula, albeit during a significantly shorter time. This creates another dilemma when interpreting the results whether a longer but lower resource utilization is more favorable than a short burst. By interpreting the results, KubeVirt appears to use most of the available resources, while OpenNebula remains idle in comparison. For this reason, this project has chosen to favor a short burst of resource utilization since it reflects the system's ability to use the resources it was allocated to its fullest potential. 

\subsection{KubeVirt Shortcomings}
It is not always the case that KubeVirt experienced a short-lived burst of utilization and OpenNebula experienced a long but low utilization. In the \textit{Live Migrate Many} tests, KubeVirt has a significantly larger CPU utilization among the workers than OpenNebula for a similar period, see Figure\ \ref{fig:migrate-many-cpu-worker}. This means, the benefit KubeVirt had in terms of utilization over duration, is not apparent in this case. This could be due to the way KubeVirt implements live migration and the possible trade-offs made since it's taking place in a Kubernetes cluster. Another explanation could be that the specific Kubernetes distribution used in the tests acts as a bottleneck. However, exploring solutions for this is outside the scope of this project.

\subsection{OpenNebula Shortcomings}
The final test in the performance analysis involved scaling a cluster whose hosting nodes had a \Gls{vm} running on it. It started by scaling a cluster from 2 to 10 worker nodes, then creating a type-1 \Gls{vm} on every worker node before scaling it down to 2 worker nodes again. This required the \Gls{vm} management system to move the \Glspl{vm} to other worker nodes, preferably those that would remain after the cluster was scaled down. As such, this imposed a challenge on the management system since a delayed scale operation could result in \Glspl{vm} being wrongfully scheduled to a node that was just about to disconnect. KubeVirt managed to complete this task without any significant overhead compared to scaling without \Glspl{vm}. However, OpenNebula was not able to complete it due to some \Glspl{vm} getting stuck on hosts that should have disconnected. Since the test was designed to disconnect the worker and then wait for each host to be empty, the OpenNebula implementation hung indefinitely. 

A possible explanation for this could be that OpenNebula attempted to move a \Gls{vm} to a host that was just about to disconnect but hadn't yet, causing the receiving host to end up in a bad state. Another explanation could be that OpenNebula required more than one control plane node to handle such bursts for migration operation since its environment only included a single control plane node. However, this is purely speculative and exploring the cause of the issue is a possible future research direction. The issue of \Glspl{vm} stuck on hosts could be resolved by manual intervention, but since this was deemed unfair to the other system, OpenNebula was disqualified from the \textit{Scale with \Glspl{vm}} tests. For this reason, OpenNebula's result was omitted from the test and only KubeVirt was evaluated.

\section{Kubernetes Overhead and Induced Load}
Throughout the results of the performance analysis, there is a pattern where OpenNebula experiences a consistently lower base overhead than KubeVirt despite of whether an operation is active or not, especially in terms of RAM utilization. Having a larger overhead means that the management system has more background processes, which is generally not desired. A clear example of this can be found in the \textit{Create VM Type 1} test and \textit{Create VM Type 4}, see Figures\ \ref{fig:create-tiny-kubevirt},\ \ref{fig:create-tiny-opennebula},\ \ref{fig:create-large-kubevirt} and\ \ref{fig:create-large-opennebula}, where KubeVirt's CPU utilization hovers just above 4\% while OpenNebula stays around 2\% utilization, and memory utilization being 8\% and 12\% respectively. This is further supported by the \textit{Live Migrate} test where their memory utilization is also 8\% and 12\% respectively. This is highly indicative that KubeVirt is more resource-intensive while not performing any operation.

\subsection{Source of Overhead}
The first possible cause of this could be tied to Kubernetes not originally being designed to manage \Glspl{vm}. Certain operations and orchestration mechanisms might not yet translate well to \Glspl{vm}, which could manifest as resource usage overhead. A Kubernetes cluster could also have services running that aid container orchestration but are not necessary for the operation performed in the tests. 
Secondly, KubeVirt might not be the culprit in the situation, and the overhead could be tied to Kubernetes itself. This could be taken a step further, where the overhead could be larger for certain Kubernetes distributions. However, this is unlikely since the Kubernetes distribution used in the tests is designed to be as lightweight as possible.
Lastly, as described in\ \ref{sec:kubevirt-technical-overview}, KubeVirt enables \Glspl{vm} to run in Kubernetes by wrapping them inside a Pod. While this has enabled seamless integration with other Kubernetes resources, it also implies there could be a layer of indirection, meaning that any traffic to and from the \Gls{vm} has extra routing steps. However, whether a Pod imposes a significant overhead for KubeVirt \Glspl{vm} is a possible future research direction. 

\subsection{Base Overhead vs Induced Load}
The flip side of the additional overhead KubeVirt inhibits is that many of the results suggest the opposite regarding the induced load, meaning the added load on the system when performing a specific operation. It can be calculated as the difference between the total utilization and the base utilization. This essentially isolates base overhead from the equation and adds another dimension to the evaluation. The induced load can be difficult to interpret for reasons previously described where there is a trade-off between a short burst of utilization and a longer small utilization. 

The results suggest that KubeVirt has a significantly lower induced load for most tests, where in certain cases OpenNebula experiences an induced load twice as large as KubeVirt. This is exemplified in the \textit{Create Many \Glspl{vm}} test where OpenNebula both takes twice as long to complete but also experiences an induced memory load twice as large as KubeVirt. This finding is essentially the opposite of the finding with base overhead, which creates another point of discussion of which factor is most prominent. From the results, it appears that induced load is a more costly factor when comparing the two systems. One example is the \textit{Migrate Many} test where OpenNebula reaches an induced load of 76\% while KubeVirt reaches just above 50\%, see Figure\ \ref{fig:migrate-many-memory-induced}. Previously, the difference between the system's base overhead was less than 10 percentage points, but this example shows an induced load with a difference of 26 percentage points. While this is indicative of KubeVirt being more scalable, it remains to be examined whether this holds for a system with many more nodes.

\subsection{Summary}
In summary, the performance analysis has shown that KubeVirt has a larger base overhead, but a significantly lower induced load for most of the tests. This suggests that KubeVirt is more scalable than OpenNebula, but could be resource-heavy in scenarios where the available hardware resources are small or where operations are few. 

\section{Jagged Disk Utilization}
Throughout the performance analysis results, there is a recurring and notable pattern specifically tied to disk utilization. The disk utilization graphs are jagged and, in many cases, do not follow the expected pattern of the specific operation performed in the test. This is particularly evident in the \textit{Create \Gls{vm} Type 4} tests, see Figures\ \ref{fig:create-large-kubevirt} and\ \ref{fig:create-large-opennebula}. 

The disk utilization for both Kubevirt and OpenNebula is initially larger than expected and does not return to the same state after the test is finished. Since the tests were run multiple times in no particular order, this likely means that noise was introduced in the measurement that caused each test to generate data points in different ranges. When the induced disk utilization was low for a particular test, background disk utilization from sources such as \Gls{os}-level operations, was more prominent in the results. By then applying a rolling mean smoothing function to the dataset, the noise was reduced, but due to the variability, some jagged features remained. 

This is further supported by the fact that the disk utilization is less jagged for tests with a larger expected disk utilization, such as the \textit{Create Many Type-1 \Glspl{vm}} tests since these tests generate a larger induced load. While the jagged disk utilization is not a problem in itself, it is important to consider when interpreting the results, especially those where disk utilization is small, as it could introduce bias unrelated to the operation performed.

\section{Mixed Opinions Using KubeVirt}
The usability analysis was designed such that it represents the tasks commonly performed by a system administrator. The results of the analysis include mixed opinions and feedback. While certain basic operations were perceived as straightforward, more complex tasks received more negative feedback. To understand this discrepancy, it is important to consider if the feedback is contradictory or if it targeted the same aspect of the technology. 

\subsection{Positive Aspects}
Starting with the positive feedback, many participants found that the \Gls{cli} tool kubectl was beneficial. This was confirmed by many participants and could be linked to previous Kubernetes experience, and could thus indicate that previous Kubernetes knowledge affects the perceived usability of KubeVirt positively. Defining \Glspl{vm} in manifests was mostly positive due to the way it contains all information regarding a \Gls{vm}, but also how it enables simpler resource management and allows external tools to integrate better. However, containing all the technical information about a \Gls{vm} in a manifest also proved to be confusing. This is not an unexpected outcome since a traditional \Gls{vm} management system often hides many configuration options from the user to improve usability. As such, it is rather a trade-off between transparency and usability. 

\subsection{KubeVirt-specific Opinions}
Mixed opinions were also received regarding management tasks. This is particularly true for live migration, which could be caused by how KubeVirt redefines a live migration operation as a declarative operation. Structuring a live migration operation as a resource, where the action is performed once the resource is created is a common pattern in Kubernetes, but the results of the usability analysis suggest this approach could be confusing.

\subsection{Insufficient Documentation}
Other negative feedback was more concerned with exploring KubeVirt outside the scope of the usability analysis. Specifically, the official KubeVirt documentation was found to be lacking practical examples of how to get started or how to debug an issue encountered with a \Gls{vm}. While evaluating documentation was not directly part of the usability analysis, it is still a significant problem for technology that involves as many user interactions as KubeVirt. 

\subsection{Summary}
In summary, the mixed responses from the usability analysis can be caused by biases among the participants regarding previous experience with either system. It could also be caused by KubeVirt being a controversial approach to managing \Glspl{vm}. A common theme for Kubernetes throughout this report is that it is known for scalability and integration, but also for having a steep learning curve. Since KubeVirt follows the design patterns of Kubernetes, it inevitably inherits both its positive and negative aspects. This poses an interesting future research direction that could explore whether the previous \Gls{vm} management system affects the perceived usability of KubeVirt.

\section{Concluding Research Questions}
The results from the performance analysis indicate both strengths and weaknesses for KubeVirt. As discussed in previous sections, KubeVirt has a larger base overhead compared to OpenNebula. However, the induced load is significantly lower for most tasks in KubeVirt compared to OpenNebula. This suggests that KubeVirt could be more efficient when performing operations and thus could be more scalable than OpenNebula. Moreover, the usability analysis indicates that KubeVirt in many cases benefits from the inherited aspects of Kubernetes, but just like Kubernetes itself, it can be perceived as complex. This section aims to summarize the results and discussion points and answer this project's research questions. 

\subsubsection*{What performance, scalability, and operational efficiencies can be gained by integrating \Glspl{vm} in Kubernetes?}
KubeVirt has been shown to inhibit a relatively large overhead while not performing any operations, evidenced by the difference in resource utilization compared to OpenNebula. This suggests that KubeVirt is not suitable for environments in which nodes are relatively small, since it might not provide performance efficiencies compared to existing \Gls{vm} management systems. 

However, KubeVirt's relatively small induced load compared to OpenNebula indicates that there exist environments in which KubeVirt does provide significant performance and, especially, scalability efficiencies. This means that given that the base overhead is not a significant part of a node's capacity, KubeVirt is a more performant and scalable alternative than OpenNebula. 

Operational efficiency comes with a similar argument. Since KubeVirt inherits Kubernetes's positive and negative aspects, there are scenarios in which KubeVirt is too complex of a system to justify potential benefits. These scenarios include organizations whose system administrators are not familiar with Kubernetes. However, given that the operators have experience with Kubernetes, the benefits of KubeVirt have shown to be substantial. This is mostly due to \Glspl{vm} following a more cloud-native workflow, supporting many integrations that might only otherwise be available for containers. One such example is GitOps, which enables KubeVirt \Glspl{vm} to be managed using Git. 

\subsubsection*{What are the current challenges and limitations in managing \Glspl{vm} and containerized applications in separate environments?}
The literature study includes several studies to explore the current challenges and limitations of managing \Glspl{vm} and containers in separate environments. It concludes that \Glsvm{vm} and container share many aspects in how they are used, such as how they both provide a virtual computing environment. This means that containers and \Glspl{vm} are often used for similar or even the same kind of workloads. 

Moreover, having \Glspl{vm} and containers in separate management platforms directly implies that the architecture is more complex and that an organization must maintain a larger skillset to manage both systems. Additionally, features and mechanisms that could have been applied for both \Glspl{vm} and containers, such as scheduling, exist in duplicates. 

Another perspective on these issues is not tied to the current form of resource management, but rather to what is not fully utilized by separating the management for \Glspl{vm} and containers. A platform such as Kubernetes provides a large variety of tools and third-party integrations that can be utilized by both \Glspl{vm} and containers. In summary, this project not only presents the benefits of integrating \Glspl{vm} and containers into a unified environment but also the benefits of integrating \Glspl{vm} into Kubernetes. 

\subsubsection*{How can system administration be simplified by running \Glspl{vm} in Kubernetes using KubeVirt?}
The literature study and usability analysis cover this research question, and they both reach similar conclusions. They suggest that the declarative approach that KubeVirt employs can improve debugging and ease of management due to how contained the information is. One such example is from the usability analysis where one participant found that the configuration files made debugging quicker. 

Furthermore, using KubeVirt could come with additional benefits due to its declarative design. For instance, managing \Glspl{vm} through manifests opens up the possibility of using GitOps, which removes the management-related burden from a user by providing version control and auditing. 

However, as mentioned throughout this report, Kubernetes still comes with a steep learning curve, and the benefits suggested here are only fully utilized if the person using is familiar with Kubernetes and its concepts. 

\chapter{Conclusions}
This project has successfully evaluated KubeVirt with Kubernetes as a \Gls{vm} management system. It used a mixed-method approach that consisted of a literature study, a performance analysis and a usability analysis to create a general image of the applicability of KubeVirt in a real-world scenario.

The literature study explored both KubeVirt and Kubernetes as technologies, but also what led to KubeVirt's creation. It found that since KubeVirt is based on Kubernetes, it inherits many of its features and workflows. This enables \Glspl{vm} created with KubeVirt to integrate well with the Kubernetes ecosystem and other Kubernetes resources, but also with tools that were otherwise only available for containers. 

The performance analysis found that KubeVirt comes with a larger base overhead than the compared traditional \Gls{vm} management system, which indicates that KubeVirt might not be suitable for all kinds of scenarios. However, it also found that KubeVirt appears to be more scalable due to its low induced load. 

The usability analysis found that users are generally keen on the idea of using a Kubernetes-based approach, but that it comes with a steep learning curve. Additionally, external resources such as documentation were found to be lacking, which indicates that KubeVirt is not yet fully developed. 

In summary, this project has shown that while KubeVirt is not suitable for all scenarios, it can provide a more scalable alternative to traditional \Gls{vm} management systems. However, since it is based on Kubernetes, it inevitably inherits its positive and negative aspects, meaning that if Kubernetes is not well received in an organization, KubeVirt probably won't be either.

\chapter{Future Work}
The discussion chapter brought up many possible future research directions, related to both performance and usability. These include exploring the source of the overhead discrepancy found in the performance analysis, and why KubeVirt experiences a larger base overhead than OpenNebula. Additionally, future work could investigate the if usability of KubeVirt is different depending on the previous \Gls{vm} management system. This could be done by conducting a usability analysis with people experienced in various systems. Lastly, since \Glspl{vm} are often used to host applications with strict security requirements, it could be an interesting future research topic to evaluate if KubeVirt with Kubernetes provides as secure environments as used today.

\section{Metrics Collector}
\label{appendix:metrics-collector}
\begin{lstlisting}[language=bash]
#!/bin/bash

interval=1
OUTPUT_FILE="./output.json"

cpu_tmp_file=$(mktemp)
ram_tmp_file=$(mktemp)
disk_tmp_file=$(mktemp)

while true; do
    if [ ! -f "./run" ]; then
        sleep $interval
        continue
    fi

    (mpstat -P ALL "$interval" 1 | awk '/^[0-9]/ {if (NR>1) print $3/100}' | jq -R -s -c 'split("\n")[1:-1]' | jq '[.[] | gsub(","; ".") | tonumber]' > "$cpu_tmp_file") &

    (free | awk '/Mem:/ {print $3/$2}' | sed 's/,/./' > "$ram_tmp_file") &

    (iostat -dx "$disk" 1 2 | awk '/Device/{flag=1; next} flag && /^[^ ]/ {util=$NF} END{print util/100}' | sed 's/,/./' > "$disk_tmp_file") &
    timestamp=$(date -u +"%Y-%m-%dT%H:%M:%SZ")

    wait

    name=$(hostname)
    cpu_core_utilization=$(cat "$cpu_tmp_file")
    cpu_utilization_avg=$(echo "$cpu_core_utilization" | jq 'add/length')
    ram_utilization=$(cat "$ram_tmp_file")
    disk_utilization=$(cat "$disk_tmp_file")

    # Create the JSON object for the current datapoint
    datapoint=$(jq -n \
                    --argjson cpuCores "$cpu_core_utilization" \
                    --argjson cpuAverage "$cpu_utilization_avg" \
                    --arg name "$name" \
                    --arg ramUtilization "$ram_utilization" \
                    --arg diskUtilization "$disk_utilization" \
                    --arg timestamp "$timestamp" \
                    '{name: $name, cpu: {cores: $cpuCores, average: $cpuAverage}, ram: ($ramUtilization | tonumber), disk: ($diskUtilization | tonumber), timestamp: $timestamp}')

    if [ ! -f "$OUTPUT_FILE" ]; then
        echo "[]" > "$OUTPUT_FILE"
    fi

    jq --argjson newData "$datapoint" '. += [$newData]' "$OUTPUT_FILE" > tmp.$$.json && mv tmp.$$.json "$OUTPUT_FILE"

    sleep $interval
done
\end{lstlisting}

\section*{Introduction}
This is a usability analysis that aims to evaluate KubeVirt by performing a set of tasks and then reflecting on the experience. Your answers will be used as a base to evaluate the usability of KubeVirt.

The following is a brief introduction to KubeVirt. If you are already familiar with KubeVirt or feel brave enough, you can skip this part.
\\~\\
KubeVirt can be seen as an extension to Kubernetes that allows \Glspl{vm} to run alongside containers. This means \Glspl{vm} can be treated as any other Kubernetes resource with its own manifests and configuration. 

Certain \Gls{vm} operations are not possible using kubectl, such as accessing the \Gls{vm} using SSH or VNC, so KubeVirt provides a complementary \Gls{cli} tool called \textit{virtctl}.

Since KubeVirt is built on top of Kubernetes, it allows \Glspl{vm} to integrate with other resources, such as Services. For instance, a \Gls{vm} can be exposed to the network using a LoadBalancer Service. However, disk management is a bit different for \Glspl{vm} than for containers, where two examples are listed below. 
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{containerDisk}: This disk is created at every boot and makes the \Gls{vm} mimic a container.
    \item \textbf{DataVolume}: This disk rerpesent a persistent disk, meaning it survives between reboots. It can be created using templates in the \Gls{vm} manifest.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Basic usage (Task 1/3)}
The first task is about creating a \Gls{vm} using KubeVirt. You will need to create a \Gls{vm} and then access it using \Gls{ssh} or \Gls{vnc}. You can use a containerDisk for simplicity.

\subsection*{Manifests}
Below is a minimal manifest for creating the \Gls{vm} using a containerDisk. The credentials for the Cirros image are user \textbf{cirros} and password \textbf{gocubsgo}.
\begin{lstlisting}[language=yaml]
    apiVersion: kubevirt.io/v1
    kind: VirtualMachine
    metadata:
      name: my-vm
    spec:
      running: true
      template:
        spec:
          domain:
            cpu:
              cores: 1
            devices:
              disks:
              - name: containerdisk
                disk:
                  bus: virtio
              interfaces:
              - name: default
                masquerade: {}
            resources:
              requests:
                memory: 128M
          networks:
          - name: default
            pod: {}
          volumes:
          - name: containerdisk
            containerDisk:
              image: quay.io/kubevirt/cirros-container-disk-demo
\end{lstlisting}

\subsubsection*{Steps}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Create a \Gls{vm}

\section*{Maintenance (Task 2/3)}
The second task is related to maintenance, where you will create a snapshot of a \Gls{vm} and then restore it. You will also perform a live migration on a \Gls{vm} to move it between two nodes. 

A live migration is a resource, like any other resource in Kubernetes, however, it does not move a \Gls{vm} by itself, and instead only tells the scheduler it is ready to move. See the References section for more information. For this use case, we want to force a move between two nodes, however, the Kubernetes scheduler is sophisticated and supports a wide range of scheduling strategies. This means we need to "cheat" a bit. We can force a move between two nodes by using the \textit{spec.template.spec.nodeSelector} in the \Gls{vm} and editing the labels of the worker nodes in the cluster. 

The snapshot operation, is simmilar to the live migration operation, done using Kubernetes resource. However, the catch here is that containerDisks cannot be snapshotted since they don't represent a persistent storage. Instead you will use a DataVolume.

\subsection*{Live Migration Manifests}
The following is a minimal manifest for creating a \Gls{vm} that runs on a node with label \textbf{name: worker-1}. The credentials for the Cirros image are user \textbf{cirros} and password \textbf{gocubsgo}.

    kind: VirtualMachine
    metadata:
      name: my-vm
    spec:
      running: true
      template:
        spec:
          nodeSelector:
            # Edit this to use a label of your choice
            my-label: some-value

              cores: 1

                  bus: virtio

            pod: {}

          - name: containerdisk

\end{lstlisting}

\begin{lstlisting}[language=yaml]

      vmiName: my-vm
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection*{Live Migration Steps}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Ensure worker node 1 has your label (Make sure to replace \texttt{my-label} and \texttt{some-value} with your own values):
\begin{lstlisting}
kubectl label node usability-worker-1 my-label=some-value
\end{lstlisting}

    \item Create a \Gls{vm} and wait for it to start. The \Gls{vm} should start on \textbf{usability-worker-1}:
\begin{lstlisting}
kubectl apply -f my-vm.yml
\end{lstlisting}

    \item (Optional) Check if the \Gls{vm} is running on worker node 1:
\begin{lstlisting}
kubectl get vmis
\end{lstlisting}

    \item Create a LiveMigration resource or use virtctl:
\begin{lstlisting}
kubectl apply -f my-vm-migration.yml
\end{lstlisting}
\begin{lstlisting}
virtctl migrate my-vm
\end{lstlisting}

    \item Add the same label to worker node 2 and remove it from worker node 1 (Make sure to replace \texttt{my-label} and \texttt{some-value} with your own values):
\begin{lstlisting}
kubectl label node usability-worker-2 my-label=some-value
kubectl label node usability-worker-1 my-label-
\end{lstlisting}

    \item (Optional) Check if the \Gls{vm} is now running on worker node 2:
\begin{lstlisting}
kubectl get vmis
\end{lstlisting}

    \item Unlabel worker node 2
\begin{lstlisting}
kubectl label node usability-worker-2 my-label-
\end{lstlisting}

    \item Delete the \Gls{vm}
\begin{lstlisting}
kubectl delete vm my-vm
\end{lstlisting}
\end{enumerate}

\begin{lstlisting}[language=yaml]

      name: my-vm-restore

        apiGroup: kubevirt.io
        kind: VirtualMachine
        name: my-vm
      virtualMachineSnapshotName: my-vm-snapshot
\end{lstlisting}

kubectl apply -f my-vm.yml

    \item Create a snapshot of the \Gls{vm}
\begin{lstlisting}

\end{lstlisting}

    \item Stop the \Gls{vm}

\end{lstlisting}

\section*{Debugging (Task 3/3)}
The third an final tasks is related to finding errors with your \Gls{vm} and resolving them. You will be given a \Gls{vm} manifest that successfully creates a KubeVirt \Gls{vm} but that is not accessible over \Gls{ssh}. You can find alternative way to enter the machine, such as through \Gls{vnc}, or go your own way. 

\subsection*{Manifests}
The following is a manifest for creating a Ubuntu \Gls{vm} that is not accessible through SSH. Credentials for the Ubuntu image should be user \textbf{cloud} and password \textbf{cloud}.
\begin{lstlisting}[language=yaml]
    apiVersion: kubevirt.io/v1
    kind: VirtualMachine
    metadata:
      name: my-vm
    spec:
      running: true
      template:
        spec:
          domain:
            devices:
              disks:
              - name: containerdisk
                disk:
                  bus: virtio
              - name: cloudinit
                disk:
                  bus: virtio
              rng: {}
            resources:
              requests:
                cpu: 2
                memory: 2Gi
          volumes:
          - name: containerdisk
            containerDisk:
              image: quay.io/containerdisks/ubuntu:22.04
          - name: cloudinit
            cloudInitNoCloud:
              userData: |-
                #cloud-config
                users:
                - name: cloud
                  # Generated with `mkpasswd -m sha-512 --rounds 4096`
                  passwd: $6$rounds=4096$abc
                  shell: /bin/bash
                  lock-passwd: false
                  chpasswd: { expire: False }
                  sudo: ALL=(ALL) NOPASSWD:ALL
                  ssh_authorized_keys:
                  - ssh-ed25519 abc
                ssh_pwauth: True
\end{lstlisting}
